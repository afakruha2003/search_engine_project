Urban Planning and Smart Cities
A smart city is an urban model that leverages technology, human capital, and governance to improve sustainability, efficiency, and social inclusion, which are considered goals for cities of the future.[1] Smart cities use digital technology to collect data and operate services.[2][3] Data is collected from citizens, devices, buildings, or cameras. Smart city applications are diverse and include, but are not limited to, traffic and transportation systems,[4] power plants, utilities, urban forestry,[5] water supply networks, waste disposal, criminal investigations, information systems, schools, libraries, hospitals, and other community services.[6][7] The foundation of a smart city is built on the integration of people, technology, and processes, which connect and interact across sectors such as healthcare, transportation, education, infrastructure, etc.[8] Smart cities are characterized by the ways in which their local governments monitor, analyze, plan, and govern the city. In a smart city, data sharing extends to businesses, citizens, and other third parties who can derive benefit from using that data.[9][10] The three largest sources of spending associated with smart cities as of 2022 were visual surveillance, public transit, and outdoor lighting.[11]

Urban planning is the technical and political process concerned with land use and the design of the urban environment. Planners balance competing interests including housing, transportation, economic development, environmental sustainability, and community well-being in growing cities.

Smart cities use digital technology, data analytics, and information systems to improve the efficiency of urban services and infrastructure. Sensors, cameras, and connected devices collect real-time data on traffic, energy consumption, waste management, and public safety.

Sustainable urban design incorporates green spaces, energy-efficient buildings, and mixed-use development to reduce environmental impact. Green roofs, urban gardens, and tree-lined streets improve air quality and reduce the urban heat island effect in densely populated areas.

Public transportation systems including buses, subways, and light rail reduce private car usage, decrease traffic congestion, and lower carbon emissions. Transit-oriented development concentrates housing and businesses around transport hubs to create walkable, connected neighborhoods.

Affordable housing is a critical challenge in many cities worldwide as housing costs rise faster than wages. Inclusive urban planning must address gentrification, homelessness, and housing inequality to ensure cities are livable and accessible for residents of all income levels.
